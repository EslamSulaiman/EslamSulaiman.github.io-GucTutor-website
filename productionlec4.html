Chapter 3: Manufacturing Processes

Material Removal Processes

Material removal processes are manufacturing operations that remove unwanted material from a starting workpiece to create a desired shape. These processes are crucial for achieving specific dimensions, surface finishes, and geometries.

Categories of Material Removal Processes:

  1. Machining (Cutting Processes): Involves the use of a sharp cutting tool to mechanically shear away material in the form of chips. This is the most common category.
  2. Abrasive Processes: Remove material by the action of hard, abrasive particles. Examples include grinding and honing.
  3. Nontraditional Processes (Advanced Machining): Use various energy forms (mechanical, thermal, electrical, chemical) to remove material, often without a sharp cutting tool. Used for materials that are difficult to machine by conventional methods.

Machining Operations

Machining involves relative motion between the workpiece and the cutting tool, and a cutting edge that separates the chip from the workpiece. Key parameters in machining include:

  • Cutting Speed (v): Primary motion that provides the energy to cut the material. Measured in meters per minute (m/min) or feet per minute (ft/min).
  • Feed (f): Secondary motion that advances the tool into the workpiece. Measured in mm/revolution or mm/tooth.
  • Depth of Cut (d): The thickness of the material removed in one pass of the tool. Measured in mm or inches.

Common Machining Operations:

  1. Turning: Performed on a lathe. The workpiece rotates, and a single-point cutting tool moves linearly to remove material from the external or internal diameter.
    • Operations: Facing, tapering, contour turning, form turning, chamfering, threading, boring, drilling, knurling.
  2. Drilling: Creates a round hole. The tool (drill bit) rotates and is fed into the stationary workpiece.
    • Operations: Through holes, blind holes, reaming (improving hole accuracy), tapping (creating internal threads), counterboring (creating a stepped hole), countersinking (creating a conical hole), spot facing (creating a flat surface around a hole).
  3. Milling: Uses a rotating multi-edge cutter to remove material from a workpiece.
    • Types:
      • Peripheral Milling (Slab Milling): The axis of the cutter is parallel to the surface being machined.
      • Face Milling: The axis of the cutter is perpendicular to the surface being machined.

Cutting Tool Materials

Cutting tools must possess specific properties to withstand the harsh conditions during machining:

  • Hardness: Significantly harder than the workpiece material at elevated temperatures.
  • Toughness and Impact Strength: Ability to absorb energy without fracturing, especially during interrupted cuts.
  • Wear Resistance: Resistance to abrasion, adhesion, and diffusion wear, ensuring tool longevity.
  • Hot Hardness: Ability to retain hardness at the high temperatures generated during cutting.

Common Tool Materials:

  1. High-Speed Steel (HSS): A common and versatile tool material. Good toughness, but limited hot hardness.
  2. Carbides (Cemented Carbides): Much harder and better hot hardness than HSS. Very common for higher cutting speeds. Often used as inserts.
  3. Ceramics: Excellent hardness and hot hardness, but brittle. Used for high-speed machining of cast iron and hardened steel.
  4. Cubic Boron Nitride (CBN): Extremely hard, second only to diamond. Used for machining hardened steels and superalloys.
  5. Diamond: The hardest known material. Used for machining non-ferrous materials, ceramics, and for fine finishing. Not suitable for ferrous metals due to chemical reaction.

Tool Geometry (Single-Point Cutting Tools)

The geometry of a cutting tool affects chip formation, heat generation, and tool life. Key angles include:

  • Rake Angle: Controls the direction of chip flow and affects cutting forces.
    • Positive Rake Angle: Reduces cutting forces, but can weaken the tool edge.
    • Negative Rake Angle: Increases strength of the cutting edge, suitable for hard materials or interrupted cuts, but increases cutting forces.
    • Zero Rake Angle: Intermediate behavior.
  • Relief/Clearance Angle: Prevents the tool flank from rubbing against the machined surface, reducing friction and heat.
  • Nose Radius: Affects surface finish and tool strength. A larger nose radius generally improves surface finish but can lead to chatter.
