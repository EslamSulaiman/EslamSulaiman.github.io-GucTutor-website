Physics 101 Lecture 3: Motion in Two Dimensions

This lecture extends the concepts of kinematics to describe motion in two dimensions, which is essential for understanding projectile motion and other complex movements.

3.1 Kinematics in Two Dimensions

Motion in two dimensions means an object moves in a plane (e.g., the x-y plane). This type of motion can be analyzed by considering the horizontal (x) and vertical (y) components of motion independently, as they are perpendicular and do not affect each other.

  • Vectors are crucial for describing two-dimensional motion, as quantities like position, velocity, and acceleration have both magnitude and direction in a plane.

3.2 Position, Displacement, Velocity, and Acceleration in 2D

3.2.1 Position Vector (\(\vec{r}\))

  • Describes the location of an object in a 2D plane relative to the origin.
  • \(\vec{r} = x\hat{i} + y\hat{j}\), where \(x\) and \(y\) are the coordinates and \(\hat{i}\) and \(\hat{j}\) are unit vectors along the x and y axes, respectively.

3.2.2 Displacement Vector (\(\Delta \vec{r}\))

  • The change in the position vector: \(\Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i = (x_f - x_i)\hat{i} + (y_f - y_i)\hat{j} = \Delta x\hat{i} + \Delta y\hat{j}\).

3.2.3 Average Velocity (\(\vec{v}_{avg}\))

  • \(\vec{v}_{avg} = \frac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}\hat{i} + \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta t}\hat{j} = v_{avg,x}\hat{i} + v_{avg,y}\hat{j}\).

3.2.4 Instantaneous Velocity (\(\vec{v}\))

  • The derivative of the position vector with respect to time: \(\vec{v} = \frac{d\vec{r}}{dt} = \frac{dx}{dt}\hat{i} + \frac{dy}{dt}\hat{j} = v_x\hat{i} + v_y\hat{j}\).
  • The magnitude of velocity is speed: \(|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}\).

3.2.5 Average Acceleration (\(\vec{a}_{avg}\))

  • \(\vec{a}_{avg} = \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\Delta v_x}{\Delta t}\hat{i} + \frac{\Delta v_y}{\Delta t}\hat{j} = a_{avg,x}\hat{i} + a_{avg,y}\hat{j}\).

3.2.6 Instantaneous Acceleration (\(\vec{a}\))

  • The derivative of the velocity vector with respect to time: \(\vec{a} = \frac{d\vec{v}}{dt} = \frac{dv_x}{dt}\hat{i} + \frac{dv_y}{dt}\hat{j} = a_x\hat{i} + a_y\hat{j}\).

3.3 Projectile Motion

Projectile motion is a common example of two-dimensional motion where an object is launched into the air and moves under the influence of gravity alone (neglecting air resistance).

  • Key Assumptions:
    • Acceleration in the horizontal (x) direction is zero (\(a_x = 0\)). This means \(v_x\) is constant.
    • Acceleration in the vertical (y) direction is due to gravity (\(a_y = -g\), where \(g \approx 9.8 \ m/s^2\) downwards).
  • The horizontal and vertical components of motion are independent. Problems are solved by applying the one-dimensional kinematic equations to each component separately.

3.3.1 Equations for Projectile Motion:

  • Horizontal Motion (constant velocity):
    • \(x = x_0 + v_{0x}t\)
    • \(v_x = v_{0x}\)
  • Vertical Motion (constant acceleration \(a_y = -g\)):
    • \(y = y_0 + v_{0y}t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\)
    • \(v_y = v_{0y} - gt\)
    • \(v_y^2 = v_{0y}^2 - 2g(y - y_0)\)

where \(v_{0x} = v_0 \cos\theta\) and \(v_{0y} = v_0 \sin\theta\), with \(v_0\) being the initial speed and \(\theta\) the launch angle with the horizontal.

Understanding motion in two dimensions, particularly projectile motion, is crucial for analyzing trajectories, designing ballistic systems, and understanding the flight of objects in various engineering applications.
