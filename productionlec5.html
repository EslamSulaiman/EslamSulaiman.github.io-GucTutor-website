Welding and Joining Processes

Welding is a material joining process in which two or more parts are coalesced at their contacting surfaces by a combination of heat and/or pressure. This results in a permanent bond between the parts, often involving melting and solidification.

Benefits of Welding:

  • Provides a permanent joint.
  • Welded joints can be stronger than the base materials.
  • Most economical way to join parts in many cases.
  • Not limited to a factory environment; can be performed in the field.

Limitations of Welding:

  • Usually performed manually, leading to high labor costs.
  • Dangerous operation due to high heat and fumes.
  • Difficult to disassemble welded joints.
  • Quality of welds can be difficult to assess.
  • Potential for residual stresses and distortion.

Types of Welding Processes (Classification)

Welding processes are primarily classified based on the mechanism of joining:

  1. Fusion Welding: Involves melting the base metals and often adding filler metal to the joint. Heat is applied to melt the edges, and upon solidification, a strong bond is formed.
    • Examples: Arc welding, resistance welding, oxyfuel welding, laser beam welding, electron beam welding.
  2. Solid-State Welding: Joining takes place without fusion (no melting) of the base metals. Pressure and/or heat (below melting point) are applied to achieve coalescence.
    • Examples: Diffusion welding, friction welding, ultrasonic welding, explosive welding.

Arc Welding Processes (Common Fusion Welding Type)

Arc welding uses an electric arc to generate the heat required for melting the metals. An electrode is used to create the arc between the electrode and the workpiece.

1. Shielded Metal Arc Welding (SMAW) - "Stick Welding"

  • Principle: Uses a consumable electrode coated with flux. The arc melts the electrode and base metal, and the flux creates a shielding gas to protect the weld pool from the atmosphere.
  • Applications: Widely used for general construction, pipe welding, and repairs.

2. Gas Metal Arc Welding (GMAW) - "MIG Welding"

  • Principle: Uses a continuously fed consumable wire electrode and an external shielding gas (e.g., argon, CO2) to protect the weld.
  • Advantages: High deposition rate, versatile, minimal post-weld cleaning, good for automation.
  • Applications: Automotive, heavy fabrication, shipbuilding.

3. Gas Tungsten Arc Welding (GTAW) - "TIG Welding"

  • Principle: Uses a non-consumable tungsten electrode and an inert shielding gas. Filler metal is added separately if needed.
  • Advantages: Produces high-quality, precise welds, suitable for thin materials and exotic metals.
  • Applications: Aerospace, medical, food processing.

4. Submerged Arc Welding (SAW)

  • Principle: Uses a continuously fed consumable electrode and a granular flux that completely covers the arc and weld pool.
  • Advantages: High deposition rates, good for thick sections, excellent weld quality, minimal fumes.
  • Applications: Heavy plate fabrication, shipbuilding, pressure vessels.

Resistance Welding (Fusion Welding - Pressure + Heat)

Resistance welding uses heat generated by electrical resistance between two contacting surfaces to achieve fusion. Pressure is also applied.

1. Resistance Spot Welding (RSW)

  • Principle: Parts are held together by electrodes, and a current is passed through to create localized melting at the interface, forming a "nugget" or spot weld.
  • Applications: Automotive body assembly, sheet metal joining.

Other Fusion Welding Processes

1. Oxyfuel Gas Welding (OFW)

  • Principle: Uses the flame produced by the combustion of oxygen and a fuel gas (e.g., acetylene) to melt the base metals. Filler metal may be used.
  • Applications: Repair work, maintenance, small-scale fabrication.

2. Electron Beam Welding (EBW)

  • Principle: Uses a highly focused beam of high-velocity electrons to melt and fuse the metals. Performed in a vacuum.
  • Advantages: Deep, narrow welds, low distortion, high purity.
  • Applications: Aerospace, nuclear, precision components.

3. Laser Beam Welding (LBW)

  • Principle: Uses a concentrated beam of coherent light (laser) as the heat source.
  • Advantages: High welding speeds, precise, low heat input, good for automation.
  • Applications: Automotive, electronics, medical devices.
