1.2 Units and Physical Quantities

Physics deals with measurable phenomena, which are described using physical quantities. Each physical quantity has a specific unit for its measurement.

  • Physical Quantities: Properties of phenomena, bodies, or substances that can be measured. Examples include length, mass, time, temperature, and electric current.
  • Units: Standard measures for physical quantities. The International System of Units (SI) is commonly used in science and engineering.

1.2.1 SI Base Units (Fundamental Units)

These are the core units from which all other units are derived:

  • Length: Meter (m)
  • Mass: Kilogram (kg)
  • Time: Second (s)
  • Electric Current: Ampere (A)
  • Thermodynamic Temperature: Kelvin (K)
  • Amount of Substance: Mole (mol)
  • Luminous Intensity: Candela (cd)

1.2.2 Derived Units

These units are formed by combining base units. Examples include:

  • Area: square meters (m²)
  • Volume: cubic meters (m³)
  • Speed: meters per second (m/s)
  • Density: kilograms per cubic meter (kg/m³)
  • Force: Newton (N), which is kg·m/s²

1.3 Vector and Scalar Quantities

Physical quantities can be classified based on whether they have direction.

  • Scalar Quantities: Quantities that have only magnitude (a numerical value and a unit). They do not have a direction.
    • Examples: Mass, time, temperature, speed, distance, energy.
  • Vector Quantities: Quantities that have both magnitude and direction. They are represented graphically by arrows, where the length of the arrow indicates magnitude and the arrowhead indicates direction.
    • Examples: Displacement, velocity, acceleration, force, momentum.

1.4 Vector Operations (Introduction)

Vectors require specific rules for addition, subtraction, and multiplication, unlike scalar quantities. Basic vector operations include:

  • Vector Addition: Can be done graphically (head-to-tail method, parallelogram method) or analytically (component method).
  • Vector Subtraction: Defined as adding the negative of a vector (e.g., A - B = A + (-B)).
  • Scalar Multiplication: Multiplying a vector by a scalar changes its magnitude but not its direction (if the scalar is positive).

Understanding these fundamental concepts of units, physical quantities, and vectors is essential for further study in mechanics and other areas of physics relevant to engineering.
