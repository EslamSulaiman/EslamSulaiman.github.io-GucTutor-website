Chapter 1: An Overview of Manufacturing

1.1 Definition of Manufacturing

Manufacturing is defined as the application of physical and/or chemical processes to alter the geometry, properties, and/or appearance of a given starting material to make parts or products. This encompasses not only the transformation of raw materials but also the joining of discrete parts into assembled products. The processes involved in manufacturing typically require a combination of various resources:

  • Machines: Equipment used to perform operations.
  • Tools: Implements attached to machines to perform specific tasks.
  • Power: Energy required to operate machines and processes.
  • Labor: Human effort and skill applied in the manufacturing process.

Beyond the physical and chemical transformations, manufacturing also integrates essential business functions vital for the entire product lifecycle. These functions ensure that products are not only made but also designed, brought to market, and delivered effectively. Key business functions include:

  • Marketing and sales to identify customer needs and promote products.
  • Order entry for processing customer requests.
  • Production planning and control for scheduling and managing manufacturing operations.
  • Manufacturing engineering for designing and optimizing production processes.
  • Logistics for managing the flow of materials and products.
  • Finance for managing monetary resources.
  • And other supporting functions.

1.2 Manufacturing Industries

Industries are broadly categorized based on their primary economic activities and the types of products or services they provide. There are three main classifications:

  1. Primary Industries: These industries are involved in the cultivation and exploitation of natural resources. Examples include agriculture (farming), mining (extraction of minerals), and fishing.
  2. Secondary Industries: These industries take the raw materials or outputs from primary industries and convert them into manufactured goods, which can be either consumer goods (for direct consumption) or capital goods (used to produce other goods). Examples include manufacturing, construction, and power generation.
  3. Tertiary Industries: This category constitutes the service sector, providing services rather than tangible goods. Examples include transportation, finance, retail trade, healthcare, and education.

In this context, manufacturing is a core component of the secondary industries. Within secondary industries, a further distinction is often made between Process Industries and Discrete Product Industries. This course, Production Technology EDPT 201, specifically focuses on the manufacturing of discrete products.

1.3 Discrete Products

Discrete products refer to individual parts and assembled products that can be identified, counted, and often disassembled. Unlike continuous materials (e.g., liquids, powders), discrete products have distinct forms and can be handled individually. Examples of discrete products include:

  • Automobiles and their components.
  • Airplanes and their structural elements.
  • Furniture.
  • Computers and electronic devices.
  • Mechanical components such as gears, shafts, and fasteners.

These products are characterized by their ability to be counted as individual units.

1.4 Production Quantity and Product Variety

The operational characteristics of a manufacturing plant are largely defined by two critical dimensions:

  1. Production Quantity (Q): This refers to the number of units of a given product produced annually within a manufacturing facility. It indicates the volume of output.
  2. Product Variety (PV): This signifies the number of different product designs or types that are produced in a plant. It reflects the diversity of the products manufactured.

There exists an inherent inverse relationship between Production Quantity (Q) and Product Variety (PV): as one increases, the other generally decreases. This relationship helps classify manufacturing operations into three general categories:

  1. Low Production Quantity:
    • Range: Typically 1 to 100 units per year.
    • Characteristics: Corresponds to very high product variety. Products in this category are often highly complex, large in size, and expensive.
    • Examples: Airplanes, large industrial turbines, specialized machinery, custom tooling.
    • Production Environment: Relies heavily on highly skilled labor and general-purpose machines that can be adapted to various tasks. Often referred to as a "Job Shop" production type, emphasizing custom work and flexibility.
  2. Medium Production Quantity:
    • Range: Typically 100 to 10,000 units per year.
    • Characteristics: Plants in this category may produce a medium quantity of one or a few products, or a range of different products in batches.
    • Examples: Components for machine tools, commercial machinery, some consumer durables (e.g., specialized appliances, furniture models).
    • Production Environment: Often employs "Batch Production" where a quantity of products is made, then the setup changes for another product. "Cellular Manufacturing" is also prevalent, where groups of machines are arranged to produce a family of similar parts efficiently.
  3. High Production Quantity:
    • Range: Typically 10,000 to millions of units per year.
    • Characteristics: Characterized by very high production quantity and low product variety. The focus is on maximizing output of a standardized product.
    • Examples: Mass-produced consumer products like automobiles, household appliances, electronic devices, or very large quantities of standardized parts such as fasteners, engine blocks, and castings.
    • Production Environment: Often referred to as "Mass Production," utilizing specialized equipment and often a less skilled labor force (compared to job shops) trained for repetitive tasks on highly automated lines.

1.5 Manufacturing Capability

The manufacturing capability of a plant encompasses its ability to perform specific processes and the inherent physical limitations of the products it can make. This includes:

  1. Technological Processing Capability: This refers to the actual set of available manufacturing processes within the plant. This could include shaping processes (e.g., casting, forming), machining processes (e.g., turning, milling), assembly processes, and various other operations. It defines what transformations the plant can perform on materials.
  2. Physical Product Limitations: These are the constraints on the attributes of the products that can be manufactured, such as their maximum and minimum size, weight limits, and the types of material properties that can be processed. For example, a plant might be equipped to handle only certain metal alloys or plastics, or only parts within a specific dimensional range.

Furthermore, manufacturing capabilities are significantly influenced by the quality and quantity of human resources available, including the skill level of the workforce, their training, and their ability to operate and maintain complex machinery and systems.

1.6 Production Facilities

The design and organization of production facilities are largely determined by the specific combination of production quantity and product variety. The general categories of production facilities, corresponding to the Q-PV relationship, include:

  • Job Shop:
    • Purpose: Designed for low production quantities and very high product variety (customized orders).
    • Equipment: Utilizes general-purpose equipment that can be reconfigured for different jobs.
    • Labor: Employs highly skilled labor capable of performing a wide range of tasks and machine setups.
    • Layout: Typically a process-oriented layout (or functional layout), where similar machines are grouped together (e.g., all lathes in one area, all milling machines in another). Workpieces move between departments as needed.
  • Batch Production:
    • Purpose: Suitable for medium quantities of a limited variety of products.
    • Equipment: Often uses a mix of specialized and general-purpose machines.
    • Labor: Involves a mix of skilled and semi-skilled labor.
    • Layout: Can have either a process-oriented or a product-oriented layout, depending on the specific batch and product characteristics.
  • Cellular Manufacturing:
    • Purpose: A hybrid approach that combines aspects of job shops and mass production, designed for medium production quantities and medium product variety. It aims to achieve some flow efficiency in a batch environment.
    • Structure: Involves grouping different types of machines (e.g., a lathe, a milling machine, a drill press) into "cells" to produce a family of parts that require similar processing steps.
    • Benefits: Reduced setup times, lower work-in-process inventory, improved quality.
  • Mass Production (Production Line):
    • Purpose: Optimized for high production quantities and very low product variety (standardized products).
    • Equipment: Characterized by specialized equipment designed for specific, often repetitive, operations.
    • Labor: Employs a less skilled workforce that performs highly specialized and repetitive tasks.
    • Layout: Primarily a product-oriented layout (or line layout), where machines are arranged sequentially according to the steps required to produce a specific product.
