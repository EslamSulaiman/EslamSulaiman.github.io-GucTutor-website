1. Higher Order Derivatives

Higher order derivatives are successive derivatives of a function. For example, the second derivative is the derivative of the first derivative, the third derivative is the derivative of the second derivative, and so on.

Notations:

First Derivative: $y^{\prime}=\frac{dy}{dx}=f^{\prime}(x)=D_x f(x)$

Second Derivative: $y^{\prime\prime}=\frac{d^{2}y}{dx^{2}}=f^{\prime\prime}(x)=D_x^{2} f(x)$

Third Derivative: $y^{\prime\prime\prime}=\frac{d^{3}y}{dx^{3}}=f^{\prime\prime\prime}(x)=D_x^{3} f(x)$

nth Derivative: $y^{(n)}=\frac{d^{n}y}{dx^{n}}=f^{(n)}(x)=D_x^{n} f(x)$

Example 1: Find the first 5 derivatives of $f(x)=x^{4}-2x^{2}+x-3$.

Solution: $f^{\prime}(x)=4x^{3}-4x+1$ $f^{\prime\prime}(x)=12x^{2}-4$ $f^{\prime\prime\prime}(x)=24x$ $f^{(4)}(x)=24$ $f^{(5)}(x)=0$

Example 2: If $y=\frac{1}{x+1}$, find $\frac{d^{2}y}{dx^{2}}$ and $\frac{d^{n}y}{dx^{n}}$.

Solution: Given $y=(x+1)^{-1}$. $\frac{dy}{dx}=-(x+1)^{-2}$ $\frac{d^{2}y}{dx^{2}}=(-)(-2)(x+1)^{-3}=2(x+1)^{-3}$ $\frac{d^{3}y}{dx^{3}}=2(-3)(x+1)^{-4}=-6(x+1)^{-4}$ $\frac{d^{4}y}{dx^{4}}=(-6)(-4)(x+1)^{-5}=24(x+1)^{-5}$ In general, $\frac{d^{n}y}{dx^{n}}=(-1)^{n}n!(x+1)^{-(n+1)}$

Example 3: If $y=\sin 3x$, find $\frac{d^{2}y}{dx^{2}}$.

Solution: $\frac{dy}{dx}=3\cos 3x$ $\frac{d^{2}y}{dx^{2}}=3(-3\sin 3x)=-9\sin 3x$

Example 4: If $y=\ln x$, find $y^{(4)}$.

Solution: $y^{\prime}=\frac{1}{x}=x^{-1}$ $y^{\prime\prime}=-x^{-2}$ $y^{\prime\prime\prime}=2x^{-3}$ $y^{(4)}=-6x^{-4}$

2. Rolle's Theorem

Let $f$ be a function that satisfies the following three hypotheses:

  1. $f$ is continuous on the closed interval $[a, b]$.
  2. $f$ is differentiable on the open interval $(a, b)$.
  3. $f(a)=f(b)$.

Then there is a number $c$ in $(a, b)$ such that $f^{\prime}(c)=0$.

Geometrical Meaning: At $x=c$, the tangent line to the curve is horizontal.

Examples:

1. Prove that between any two roots of $f(x)=\sin x$, there is a root of $f^{\prime}(x)=\cos x$.

2. Show that the equation $x^{3}+x-1=0$ has exactly one real root.

3. Mean Value Theorem (MVT)

Let $f$ be a function that satisfies the following two hypotheses:

  1. $f$ is continuous on the closed interval $[a, b]$.
  2. $f$ is differentiable on the open interval $(a, b)$.

Then there is a number $c$ in $(a, b)$ such that $$f^{\prime}(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$$ or $f(b)-f(a)=f^{\prime}(c)(b-a)$.

Geometrical Meaning: There is at least one point $c$ in $(a, b)$ at which the tangent line is parallel to the secant line passing through $(a, f(a))$ and $(b, f(b))$.

Special Case: If $f(a)=f(b)$, the Mean Value Theorem reduces to Rolle's Theorem.

Example 1: Does the Mean Value Theorem apply to $f(x)=|x|$ on the interval $[-1, 2]$? If yes, find $c$.

Solution: $f(x)=|x|$ is continuous on $[-1, 2]$. However, $f(x)=|x|$ is not differentiable at $x=0$, which is in the interval $(-1, 2)$. Therefore, the Mean Value Theorem does not apply.

Example 2: Find all values of $c$ in the interval $(0, 2)$ such that $f^{\prime}(c)=\frac{f(2)-f(0)}{2-0}$ for $f(x)=x^{3}-x$.

Solution: $f(x)=x^{3}-x$ is continuous on $[0, 2]$ and differentiable on $(0, 2)$ (since it's a polynomial). $f^{\prime}(x)=3x^{2}-1$ $f(0)=0^{3}-0=0$ $f(2)=2^{3}-2=8-2=6$ $\frac{f(2)-f(0)}{2-0}=\frac{6-0}{2}=3$ Now, set $f^{\prime}(c)=3$: $3c^{2}-1=3$ $3c^{2}=4$ $c^{2}=\frac{4}{3}$ $c=\pm\sqrt{\frac{4}{3}}=\pm\frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}$ Since $c$ must be in the interval $(0, 2)$, we choose $c=\frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}$.

4. Generalized Mean Value Theorem (GMVT) or Cauchy's Mean Value Theorem

If functions $f$ and $g$ are continuous on $[a, b]$ and differentiable on $(a, b)$, and $g^{\prime}(x)\ne0$ for all $x$ in $(a, b)$, then there exists a number $c$ in $(a, b)$ such that: $$\frac{f^{\prime}(c)}{g^{\prime}(c)}=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{g(b)-g(a)}$$

Note: If $g(x)=x$, then $g^{\prime}(x)=1$. Substituting this into GMVT gives the regular MVT. This means MVT is a special case of GMVT.
