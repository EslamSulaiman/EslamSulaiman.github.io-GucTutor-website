Production Technology: Lecture 2 - Measurement

Introduction to Measurement

[cite_start]

Measurement is a critical aspect of production technology, essential for quality control and ensuring products meet precise specifications[cite: 9].

Need for Inspection:

[cite_start]

Measuring is important in the industrial world to ensure product quality and adherence to design specifications[cite: 9].

Standardization:

Standardization is the process of conforming to established rules or measures. It's crucial in modern industry to ensure interchangeability of products between different manufacturers and countries. [cite_start]Standards are set by organizations like the International Standards Organization (ISO), British Standards Institution (BSI), Deutsches Institut f√ºr Normung (DIN), and American National Standards Institute (ANSI)[cite: 11, 12, 13].

Precision and Accuracy:

  • Accuracy: Refers to how close a measured value is to the true or known value. [cite_start]For example, if the actual weight of a substance is 10 kg, but your measurement is consistently 3.2 kg, it is not accurate[cite: 15, 16].
  • Precision: Refers to the closeness of two or more measurements to each other, indicating reproducibility. [cite_start]If you weigh a substance five times and get 3.2 kg each time, your measurement is very precise, even if not accurate[cite: 17].
[cite_start]

It's possible to have measurements that are accurate and precise, precise but not accurate, or neither accurate nor precise[cite: 19].

Calibration:

[cite_start]

Calibration is the procedure where a measurement instrument is checked against a known standard to ensure its accuracy[cite: 20].

Measurement Methods:

  • Direct Measurement: The value of a quantity is obtained directly by comparing the unknown with a standard, without mathematical calculations. [cite_start]An example is measuring thickness using a Vernier Caliper[cite: 21, 22].
  • Indirect Measurement: Several related parameters are measured directly, and then the desired value is determined using a mathematical relationship. [cite_start]An example is measuring density by measuring mass and geometrical dimensions[cite: 22, 23].

Direct Measuring Tools:

[cite_start]

Common direct measuring tools include Workshop Steel Rulers, Vernier Calipers, Micrometers, Simple Workshop Protractors, Vernier Protractors, and Try Squares[cite: 24].

1. Workshop Steel Ruler:

    [cite_start]
  • Typically has inch and centimeter divisions[cite: 25].
    • [cite_start]
  • Can measure down to 1 mm[cite: 25].
    • [cite_start]
  • Often has conversion charts on the back[cite: 25].

2. Vernier Caliper:

[cite_start]

A precision instrument capable of more accurate measurements than a ruler[cite: 26, 27, 28].

    [cite_start]
  • Can have different precisions, such as 0.1 mm, 0.05 mm, or 0.02 mm[cite: 28].
    • [cite_start]
  • Example Reading: The PDF includes examples where readings of Vernier Calipers result in values like 106.5 mm and 44.6 mm[cite: 31].

3. Micrometer:

[cite_start]

Used for even higher precision measurements of small lengths, thicknesses, and diameters[cite: 32, 33].

    [cite_start]
  • Example Reading: An example reading shown in the lecture yields 7.38 mm (composed of 7 mm from the main scale, 0 mm from the thimble scale main divisions, and 0.38 mm from the thimble scale subdivisions)[cite: 34]. [cite_start]Another example results in 3.77 mm[cite: 35].

4. Protractors:

    [cite_start]
  • Simple Workshop Protractor: Used for general angle measurements[cite: 24].
    • [cite_start]
  • Vernier Bevel Protractor: Offers higher precision for angle measurements, utilizing a vernier scale[cite: 24, 28].
