CHEM 102 Lecture 3: Chemical Bonding

This lecture introduces the fundamental concepts of chemical bonding, explaining why atoms form bonds and the different types of bonds that result in the formation of compounds.

3.1 Why Do Atoms Form Bonds?

Atoms form chemical bonds to achieve a more stable electron configuration, typically resembling that of a noble gas (an octet of valence electrons, or a duet for hydrogen and helium). This tendency is known as the **octet rule**. The formation of bonds lowers the potential energy of the atoms involved, making the resulting compound more stable.

3.2 Types of Chemical Bonds

The main types of chemical bonds are ionic bonds and covalent bonds.

3.2.1 Ionic Bonds

  • Formation: Occur between a metal and a non-metal. They involve the **transfer** of electrons from one atom to another.
  • Mechanism: The metal atom loses one or more valence electrons to become a positively charged ion (cation), and the non-metal atom gains these electrons to become a negatively charged ion (anion).
  • Attraction: The resulting oppositely charged ions are attracted to each other by electrostatic forces, forming a strong ionic bond.
  • Properties of Ionic Compounds: Typically form crystalline solids, have high melting and boiling points, and conduct electricity when molten or dissolved in water.
  • Example: Sodium Chloride (NaCl), where Na transfers an electron to Cl.

3.2.2 Covalent Bonds

  • Formation: Occur between two non-metal atoms. They involve the **sharing** of electrons between atoms.
  • Mechanism: Atoms share electrons to achieve a stable electron configuration (usually an octet). The shared electrons are attracted to the nuclei of both atoms, holding them together.
  • Types of Covalent Bonds:
    • Single Bond: Two atoms share one pair of electrons.
    • Double Bond: Two atoms share two pairs of electrons.
    • Triple Bond: Two atoms share three pairs of electrons.
  • Polarity: Covalent bonds can be polar (unequal sharing of electrons due to difference in electronegativity) or nonpolar (equal sharing).
  • Properties of Covalent Compounds (Molecules): Can exist as solids, liquids, or gases, generally have lower melting and boiling points than ionic compounds, and are typically poor conductors of electricity.

3.3 Metallic Bonds (Brief Mention)

Metallic bonds are found in metals and alloys. They involve a "sea" of delocalized valence electrons shared among a lattice of positively charged metal ions. This model explains properties like electrical conductivity, malleability, and ductility of metals.

Understanding chemical bonding is fundamental to predicting the properties of substances and designing new materials in engineering.
