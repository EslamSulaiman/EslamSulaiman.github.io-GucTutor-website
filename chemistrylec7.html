CHEM 102 Lecture 7: Materials Chemistry

This lecture introduces the vast field of engineering materials, crucial for understanding the properties and applications of substances in various industries.

Introduction to Engineering Materials

Engineering materials are fundamental to modern technology and production, with properties determining their vast applications.

Types of Materials:

  • Classical Materials:
    • **Metals:** Known for excellent conductivity, strength, and ductility.
      Example: Steel used in building structures, Copper in electrical wiring.
    • **Ceramics:** Typically brittle, high melting points, and good insulators.
      Example: Pottery, bricks, and specialized refractories.
    • **Polymers:** Large molecules, often flexible and lightweight.
      Example: Various plastics (like PVC pipes, water bottles), rubber.
    • **Semiconductors:** Materials with electrical conductivity between conductors and insulators.
      Example: Silicon (Si) chips in computers and electronic devices.
  • Advanced Materials: Include Electronic Materials (e.g., for microchips) and Composite Materials (combining two or more distinct materials).
    Example: Fiberglass (glass fibers in a polymer matrix), carbon fiber composites used in aircraft.
  • Materials of the Future: Emerging materials with unique properties.
    Example: Nanomaterials (materials engineered at the nanoscale), Smart Materials (e.g., shape-memory alloys that return to a pre-set shape).

Periodic Table and Elements Classification:

The periodic table is an indispensable tool for classifying elements and predicting their material behavior.

  • **Metals:** Form the largest group, approximately 91 out of 120 known elements. They are typically lustrous, malleable, and good conductors.
    Example: Iron (Fe), Copper (Cu), Aluminum (Al).
  • **Nonmetals:** About 18 elements, often insulators, can be gases, liquids, or brittle solids.
    Example: Oxygen (O), Nitrogen (N), Chlorine (Cl).
  • **Metalloids:** Possess properties intermediate between metals and nonmetals, often acting as semiconductors.
    Example: Boron (B), Silicon (Si), Germanium (Ge).

Chemical Bonding:

The type of bond between atoms profoundly influences the material's properties.

  • **Ionic Bonding:** Occurs between a metal and a non-metal, involving the transfer of electrons to form oppositely charged ions that attract each other.
    Example: Sodium (Na) donates an electron to Chlorine (Cl) to form $\text{Na}^+$ and $\text{Cl}^-$, resulting in Sodium Chloride (NaCl), or table salt.
  • **Covalent Bonding:** Occurs typically between two non-metals, involving the sharing of electrons to achieve stable electron configurations.
    Example: Two Oxygen atoms sharing electrons to form an $\text{O}_2$ molecule (oxygen gas), or Hydrogen and Oxygen sharing electrons in a water molecule ($\text{H}_2\text{O}$).
  • **Metallic Bonding:** Occurs between metal atoms, where valence electrons are delocalized and shared among a lattice of positive metal ions, forming an "electron sea." This explains metals' excellent conductivity and malleability.
    Example: The bonds within a solid piece of copper wire, allowing electrons to flow freely.

Materials in Context & Historical Ages:

Elements and materials are abundant in Earth's crust and atmosphere, playing a central role in human technological advancement throughout history.

  • Occurrence: Silicon (Si) makes up about 28% of Earth's crust by weight, while Iron (Fe) is about 5%.
  • Material Ages:
    • Stone Age: Characterized by the use of stone tools.
    • Bronze Age: Marked by the discovery and widespread use of bronze (an alloy of copper and tin).
    • Iron Age: Defined by the dominant use of iron for tools and weapons, due to its greater strength.
    • Silicon Age: Our modern era, driven by the development of semiconductors and integrated circuits.
    • Nano Age: The current frontier, focusing on materials designed and manipulated at the nanoscale for novel properties and applications.

Properties and Applications of Metals:

Metals are indispensable due to their unique combination of physical and chemical properties.

  • **Lustrous:** They have a characteristic shine.
  • **Good Conductors:** Excellent conductors of both heat and electricity.
    Example: Copper (Cu) is widely used for electrical wiring due to its high electrical conductivity.
  • **High Melting Points & Density:** Generally solid at room temperature with high melting points and are dense.
  • **Malleability:** The ability to be hammered or pressed into thin sheets without breaking.
    Example: Aluminum (Al) foil and gold leaf are prime examples of malleable metals.
  • **Ductility:** The ability to be drawn into thin wires without losing strength.
    Example: Copper (Cu) is easily drawn into wires for cables, and gold is highly ductile.
  • **Sonorous:** Produce a clear, ringing sound when struck.

Metallic Materials: Can be pure elemental metals or alloys.

  • Examples of Alloys (mixtures of metals):
    • **Bronze:** An alloy primarily of Copper (Cu) and Tin (Sn).
    • **Brass:** An alloy of Copper (Cu) and Zinc (Zn).
    • **Steel:** An alloy primarily of Iron (Fe) and Carbon (C), known for its strength.
    • **Solder:** A low-melting point alloy, typically of Lead (Pb) and Tin (Sn), used for joining metals.

Occurrence and Extraction of Metals:

Most metals are found in nature as part of minerals, requiring extraction and refining processes.

  • **Mineral:** A naturally occurring solid, inorganic substance with a definite chemical composition and crystalline structure.
  • **Ore:** A mineral or rock containing a valuable metal (or metals) that can be extracted profitably.
  • Examples of Ores and Metals:
    • **Hematite ($\text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3$) & Magnetite ($\text{Fe}_3\text{O}_4$):** Common iron ores from which Iron (Fe) is extracted.
    • **Bauxite ($\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3 \cdot \text{n}\text{H}_2\text{O}$) & Corundum ($\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3$):** Primary ores from which Aluminum (Al) is extracted.
