8. Hyperbolic Functions & Their Inverse Functions

This section explores the meaning, definition, and applications of hyperbolic functions, simulating trigonometric functions.

Hyperbolic Sine of x: $sinh~x=\frac{e^{x}-e^{-x}}{2}$

  • Domain ($D_f$): $\mathfrak{R}$ (all real numbers)
  • Range ($R_f$): $\mathfrak{R}$ (all real numbers)
  • It is a 1-1 function.
  • $Sin~h(0)=0$

Hyperbolic Cosine of x: $cosh~x=\frac{e^{x}+e^{-x}}{2}$

  • Domain ($D_f$): $\mathfrak{R}$ (all real numbers)
  • Range ($R_f$): $[1,\infty[$ (all real numbers greater than or equal to 1)
  • It is not a 1-1 function.
  • $cosh(0)=1$

The Remaining Hyperbolic Functions

  • Hyperbolic Tangent: $tanh~x=\frac{sinh~x}{cosh~x}=\frac{e^{x}-e^{-x}}{e^{x}+e^{-x}}$
    • Range: $-1 < tanh~x < 1$
  • Hyperbolic Cotangent: $coth~x=\frac{cosh~x}{sinh~x}=\frac{e^{x}+e^{-x}}{e^{x}-e^{-x}}$
  • Hyperbolic Secant: $sech~x=\frac{1}{cosh~x}=\frac{2}{e^{x}+e^{-x}}$
    • Range: $0 < sech~x \le 1$
  • Hyperbolic Cosecant: $csch~x=\frac{1}{sinh~x}=\frac{2}{e^{x}-e^{-x}}$

Basic Identities for Hyperbolic Functions

These identities can be proven using the basic definitions of $sinh~x$ and $cosh~x$.

  • $cosh^{2}x-sinh^{2}x=1$
  • $sinh~2x=2~sinh~x~cosh~x$
  • $cosh~2x=cosh^{2}x+sinh^{2}x$
  • $cosh^{2}x=\frac{cosh~2x+1}{2}$
  • $sinh^{2}x=\frac{cosh~2x-1}{2}$
  • $tanh^{2}x=1-sech^{2}x$
  • $coth^{2}x=1+csch^{2}x$

Proof Example ($cosh^{2}(x)-sinh^{2}(x)=1$): $LHS = cosh^{2}(x)-sinh^{2}(x)$ $=(\frac{e^{x}+e^{-x}}{2})^{2}-(\frac{e^{x}-e^{-x}}{2})^{2}$ $=\frac{1}{4}(e^{2x}+2+e^{-2x})-\frac{1}{4}(e^{2x}-2+e^{-2x})$ $=\frac{1}{4}(2+2) = 1 = RHS$

Inverse Hyperbolic Functions

It is important to remember the graphs of all inverse hyperbolic functions.

  • $sech^{-1}x=cosh^{-1}\frac{1}{x}$
  • $csch^{-1}x=sinh^{-1}\frac{1}{x}$
  • $coth^{-1}x=tanh^{-1}\frac{1}{x}$

Key Relations to Prove (Homework)

  • $sinh^{-1}x=ln(x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1})$
  • $cosh^{-1}x=ln(x+\sqrt{x^{2}-1}),x\ge1$
  • $tanh^{-1}x=\frac{1}{2}ln\frac{1+x}{1-x},-1

Proof Example ($sinh^{-1}(x)=ln(x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1})$): Let $y=sinh^{-1}x \Rightarrow x=sinh~y$ By definition of $sinh~y$, $x=\frac{e^{y}-e^{-y}}{2}$ Multiply by $e^y$: $2xe^{y}=e^{2y}-1$ Rearrange into a quadratic equation for $e^y$: $(e^{y})^{2}-2xe^{y}-1=0$ Using the quadratic formula: $e^{y}=\frac{2x\pm\sqrt{4x^{2}+4}}{2} = \frac{2x\pm2\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}{2}$ Thus, $e^{y}=x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1}$ (the negative sign is rejected because $e^y$ must be positive) Taking the natural logarithm of both sides: $y=ln(x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1})$ Therefore, $sinh^{-1}x=ln(x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1})$

Formal Definition of Limit

We say that the limit of $f(x)$ equals $L$ as $x$ approaches $a$, if the values of $x$ are sufficiently close to $a$ on either side, but not necessarily equal to $a$, causing the values of $f(x)$ to be very close to a unique number $L$.

Techniques of Limits

  1. Direct Substitution: This is the first method to try.
  2. If the limit results in an indeterminate form (e.g., $\frac{0}{0}$):
    1. Cancel the zero factor by:
      1. Factorizing both numerator and denominator.
      2. Multiplying by the conjugate.
    2. This also applies to cases involving "tan".
  3. Squeezing "Sandwich" Theorem
  4. Limits at infinity
  5. L'Hopital's Rule (to be covered after differentiation)

Note: The value of the function at a specific point (e.g., $x=1$) does not affect the value of the limit as $x$ approaches that point.
