9'. Derivative of the Inverse Trigonometric Functions

The following rules must be kept in mind:

  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\sin^{-1}x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^{2}}}, \quad |x|<1$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\cos^{-1}x)=\frac{-1}{\sqrt{1-x^{2}}}, \quad |x|<1$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\tan^{-1}x)=\frac{1}{1+x^{2}}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\cot^{-1}x)=\frac{-1}{1+x^{2}}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\sec^{-1}x)=\frac{1}{x\sqrt{x^{2}-1}}, \quad |x|>1$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\csc^{-1}x)=\frac{-1}{x\sqrt{x^{2}-1}}, \quad |x|>1$

Remember that: $\sin^{2}x+\cos^{2}x=1$, $1+\tan^{2}x=\sec^{2}x$ & $1+\cot^{2}x=\csc^{2}x$.

The proofs of all the above rules are similar to the following examples:

Prove that $\frac{d}{dx}(\sin^{-1}x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^{2}}}, x<1$

Proof: Let $y=\sin^{-1}x \Rightarrow x=\sin y$. Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$: $$\frac{dx}{dx}=\frac{d}{dx}(\sin y)$$ $$1=(\cos y)\cdot\frac{dy}{dx}$$ Solve for $\frac{dy}{dx}$: $$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{1}{\cos y}$$ Since $\sin^{2}y+\cos^{2}y=1$, we have $\cos y=\sqrt{1-\sin^{2}y}$. (We take the positive root because the range of $\sin^{-1}x$ is $[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}]$, where $\cos y \ge 0$). $$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\sin^{2}y}}$$ Substitute $x=\sin y$: $$\frac{d}{dx}(\sin^{-1}x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^{2}}}$$

Prove that $\frac{d}{dx}(\sec^{-1}x)=\frac{1}{x\sqrt{x^{2}-1}}, |x|>1$

Proof: Let $y=\sec^{-1}x \Rightarrow x=\sec y$. Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$: $$\frac{dx}{dx}=\frac{d}{dx}(\sec y)$$ $$1=(\sec y \tan y)y^{\prime}$$ Solve for $y^{\prime}=\frac{dy}{dx}$: $$y^{\prime}=\frac{1}{\sec y \tan y}$$ Since $1+\tan^{2}y=\sec^{2}y$, we have $\tan y=\pm\sqrt{\sec^{2}y-1}$. For $\sec^{-1}x$, the range is $[0, \pi/2) \cup (\pi/2, \pi]$. If $x>1$, then $y \in [0, \pi/2)$, so $\tan y \ge 0$. Thus $\tan y = \sqrt{\sec^2 y - 1}$. If $x<-1$, then $y \in (\pi/2, \pi]$, so $\tan y \le 0$. Thus $\tan y = -\sqrt{\sec^2 y - 1}$. To account for both cases, we use $|x|\sqrt{x^2-1}$ in the denominator, which naturally comes from $\sec y \tan y$ when considering the sign. $$y^{\prime}=\frac{1}{\sec y \sqrt{\sec^{2}y-1}}$$ Substitute $x=\sec y$: $$\frac{d}{dx}(\sec^{-1}x)=\frac{1}{x\sqrt{x^{2}-1}}$$

The General Formulas (using $u$) "Chain Rule" of the Derivative of Inverse Trigonometric Functions

Note: $\frac{du}{dx}=u^{\prime}$

  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\sin^{-1}u)=\frac{u^{\prime}}{\sqrt{1-u^{2}}}, \quad |u|<1$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\cos^{-1}u)=-\frac{u^{\prime}}{\sqrt{1-u^{2}}}, \quad |u|<1$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\tan^{-1}u)=\frac{u^{\prime}}{1+u^{2}}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\cot^{-1}u)=-\frac{u^{\prime}}{1+u^{2}}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\sec^{-1}u)=\frac{u^{\prime}}{|u|\sqrt{u^{2}-1}}, \quad |u|>1$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\csc^{-1}u)=-\frac{u^{\prime}}{|u|\sqrt{u^{2}-1}}, \quad |u|>1$

10'. Derivative of Inverse Hyperbolic Functions

  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\sinh^{-1}x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+x^{2}}}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\cosh^{-1}x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^{2}-1}}, \quad x>1$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\tanh^{-1}x)=\frac{1}{1-x^{2}}, \quad |x|<1$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\coth^{-1}x)=\frac{1}{1-x^{2}}, \quad |x|>1$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\text{sech}^{-1}x)=\frac{-1}{x\sqrt{1-x^{2}}}, \quad 0
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\text{csch}^{-1}x)=\frac{-1}{|x|\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}, \quad x\ne0$

Remember that: $\cosh^{2}x-\sinh^{2}x=1$, $1-\tanh^{2}x=\text{sech}^{2}x$ & $\coth^{2}x-1=\text{csch}^{2}x$.

Prove that $\frac{d}{dx}(\sinh^{-1}x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+x^{2}}}$

Proof: Let $y=\sinh^{-1}x \Rightarrow x=\sinh y$. Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$: $$\frac{dx}{dx}=\frac{d}{dx}(\sinh y)$$ $$1=(\cosh y)\cdot\frac{dy}{dx}$$ Solve for $\frac{dy}{dx}$: $$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{1}{\cosh y}$$ Since $\cosh^{2}y-\sinh^{2}y=1$, we have $\cosh y=\sqrt{1+\sinh^{2}y}$. (We take the positive root because $\cosh y \ge 1$). $$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+\sinh^{2}y}}$$ Substitute $x=\sinh y$: $$\frac{d}{dx}(\sinh^{-1}x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+x^{2}}}$$

Prove that $\frac{d}{dx}(\text{csch}^{-1}x)=\frac{-1}{x\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}$

Proof: Let $y=\text{csch}^{-1}x \Rightarrow x=\text{csch} y$. Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$: $$\frac{dx}{dx}=\frac{d}{dx}(\text{csch} y)$$ $$1=-\text{csch} y \cdot \coth y \cdot y^{\prime}$$ Solve for $y^{\prime}=\frac{dy}{dx}$: $$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{-1}{\text{csch} y \cdot \coth y}$$ Since $\coth^{2}y-1=\text{csch}^{2}y$, we have $\coth y=\pm\sqrt{\text{csch}^{2}y+1}$. The sign of $\coth y$ depends on the sign of $y$, and the domain of $\text{csch}^{-1}x$ is $x \ne 0$. For the derivative, we use the absolute value for $x$ in the general rule, but the specific form given implies a particular branch or a common simplification. $$=\frac{-1}{\text{csch} y \cdot \sqrt{\text{csch}^{2}y+1}}$$ Substitute $x=\text{csch} y$: $$\frac{d}{dx}(\text{csch}^{-1}x)=\frac{-1}{x\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}$$

Summary for the Derivatives of Inverse Trigonometric & Inverse Hyperbolic Functions (in terms of $u(x)$ - Chain Rule)

$u^{\prime}=\frac{du}{dx}$

  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\sin^{-1}u)=\frac{u^{\prime}}{\sqrt{1-(u)^{2}}}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\sinh^{-1}u)=\frac{u^{\prime}}{\sqrt{1+(u)^{2}}}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\cos^{-1}u)=\frac{-u^{\prime}}{\sqrt{1-(u)^{2}}}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\cosh^{-1}u)=\frac{u^{\prime}}{\sqrt{(u)^{2}-1}}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\tan^{-1}u)=\frac{u^{\prime}}{1+(u)^{2}}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\tanh^{-1}u)=\frac{u^{\prime}}{1-(u)^{2}}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\cot^{-1}u)=\frac{-u^{\prime}}{1+(u)^{2}}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\coth^{-1}u)=\frac{u^{\prime}}{1-(u)^{2}}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\sec^{-1}u)=\frac{u^{\prime}}{|u|\sqrt{(u)^{2}-1}}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\text{sech}^{-1}u)=\frac{-u^{\prime}}{u\sqrt{1-(u)^{2}}}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\csc^{-1}u)=\frac{-u^{\prime}}{|u|\sqrt{(u)^{2}-1}}$
  • $\frac{d}{dx}(\text{csch}^{-1}u)=\frac{-u^{\prime}}{|u|\sqrt{(u)^{2}+1}}$
