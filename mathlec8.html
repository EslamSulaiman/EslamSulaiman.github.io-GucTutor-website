Chapter 2: Applications of Limits: Continuity of Functions

Continuity at a Point

Definition:

  • Interior Point: A function $y=f(x)$ is continuous at an interior point $c$ of its domain if $lim_{x\rightarrow c}f(x)=f(c)$.
  • Endpoint: A function $y=f(x)$ is continuous at a left endpoint $a$ or a right endpoint $b$ of its domain if $lim_{x\rightarrow a^{+}}f(x)=f(a)$ or $lim_{x\rightarrow b^{-}}f(x)=f(b),$ respectively.

Continuity Test

A function $f(x)$ is continuous at $x=c$ if and only if it meets the following three conditions:

  1. $f(c)$ exists (c lies in the domain of f)
  2. $lim_{x\rightarrow c}f(x)$ exists (f has a limit as $x\rightarrow c$)
  3. $lim_{x\rightarrow c}f(x)=f(c)$ (the limit equals the function value)

Example: Computational Example

Discuss the continuity of the function $f(x)=e^{-|x|}$ at the point $x=0$.

Solution: The given function can be redefined as: $f(x)=e^{-|x|}=\begin{cases}e^{x},&if~x<0\\ e^{-x},&if~x\ge0\end{cases}$

At $x=0$, we test the three conditions of continuity:

  1. $f(0)=e^{0}=1$, by the definition of the function.
  2. We study the limit of the function as x approaches 0: $lim_{x\rightarrow0^{-}}f(x)=lim_{x\rightarrow0^{-}}e^{x}=1,$ $lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}f(x)=lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}e^{-x}=1$ $\Rightarrow lim_{x\rightarrow0}f(x)=1$
  3. Since $lim_{x\rightarrow0}f(x)=1=f(0),$ it follows that $f(x)$ is continuous at $x=0$.

Relation between Limits and Continuity

A function is not continuous at a point $x=a,$ if one of the following happens:

  • $f(a)$ is not defined.
  • $f(a)$ is defined, BUT, $lim_{x\rightarrow a} f(x)$ does not exist.
  • $f(a)$ is defined AND $lim_{x\rightarrow a} f(x)$ exists, BUT they are Not equal.

Types of Discontinuities

(1) Removable Discontinuity

A function $f$ is said to have a removable discontinuity at a point $x=a$, if the limit of $f$ exists as $x$ tends to $a$, such that either $f$ is not defined at $x=a$ or defined differently.

Examples of Removable Discontinuities at $x=0$:

  • Function: $f(x)=\begin{cases}x+1&if~x\ne0\\ 2&if~x=0\end{cases}$ Here, $f(0)=2$ is defined, but $lim_{x\rightarrow0}f(x)=1$, so the limit does not equal the function value.
  • Function: $f(x)=\frac{sin~x}{x}$ Here, $f(0)$ is not defined, but $lim_{x\rightarrow0}\frac{sin~x}{x}=1$.

(2) Jump Discontinuity

A function has a jump discontinuity at a point $x=a$, if both the left limit and the right limit at $x=a$ exist but they are not equal.

Example: $f(x)=\begin{cases}0,&x<0\\ 1,&x\ge0\end{cases}$ At $x=0$, $lim_{x\rightarrow0^{-}}f(x)=0$ and $lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}f(x)=1$. Since these are not equal, $f(x)$ has a jump discontinuity at $x=0$.

(3) Infinite Discontinuity

A function has an infinite discontinuity at a point $x=a$, if at least one of the left or right sided limits (or both) at $x=a$ equals $\pm\infty$.

Example: $g(x)=\frac{1}{(x+3)^{2}}$ At $x=-3$, $lim_{x\rightarrow-3}\frac{1}{(x+3)^{2}}=lim_{x\rightarrow-3^{\prime}}\frac{1}{(x+3)^{2}}=\infty$. Thus, $g(x)$ has an infinite discontinuity at $x=-3$.

Another Example: $f(x)=[\begin{matrix}1,&x\le0\\ \frac{1}{x},&x>0\end{matrix}]$ At $x=0$, $lim_{x\rightarrow0^{-}}f(x)=1$ and $lim_{x\rightarrow0^{+}}f(x)=\infty$. Thus, $f(x)$ has an infinite discontinuity at $x=0$.

Summary of the Types of Discontinuities

  • If $lim_{x\rightarrow a}f(x)$ exists: At $x=a$, $f$ has a **Removable discontinuity**. This occurs whether $f(a)$ is defined or not, or defined differently.
  • If $lim_{x\rightarrow a^{+}}f(x)=L$ (a number) and $lim_{x\rightarrow a^{-}}f(x)=M$ (a number), and $L\ne M$: At $x=a$, $f$ has a **Jump discontinuity**.
  • If one of the limits $lim_{x\rightarrow a^{+}}f(x)$ or $lim_{x\rightarrow a^{-}}f(x)$ equals $\pm\infty$: At $x=a$, $f$ has an **Infinite discontinuity**.

Continuity on a Domain

A function is continuous on a closed interval $[a, b]$ if it is continuous at all interior points as well as continuous at the two-end points $a, b$.

This involves understanding continuity from the right at the left endpoint ($a$), two-sided continuity at interior points ($c$), and continuity from the left at the right endpoint ($b$).

Properties of Continuous Functions

If the functions $f$ and $g$ are continuous at $x=c$, then the following combinations are continuous at $x=c$:

  1. Sums: $f+g$
  2. Differences: $f-g$
  3. Products: $f \cdot g$
  4. Constant multiples: $k \cdot f$, for any number $k$
  5. Quotients: $f/g$ provided $g(c) \ne 0$
  6. Powers: $f^{r/s}$, provided it is defined on an open interval containing $c$, where $r$ and $s$ are integers

Any polynomial function is continuous on $\mathfrak{R}$ (the whole real line).

Theorem 10: Composite of Continuous Functions

If $f$ is continuous at $c$ and $g$ is continuous at $f(c)$, then the composite $g \circ f$ is continuous at $c$.

The Intermediate Value Theorem (I.V.T.)

Notice that we can have more than one value of $c$.

Importance of the continuity of the function: The opposite figure shows that if the function is discontinuous at at least one point, then the conclusion of the theorem may be false.

Important Application: “Corollary of the I.V.T.”

If $f(x)$ is:

  1. continuous on $[a, b]$, and
  2. $f(a)$ and $f(b)$ have different signs.
Then, there is at least one point $c$ in $]a, b[$ for which $f(c) = 0$.

Example:

Show that the equation: $x^3 – x – 1 = 0$ has at least one solution between $x = 1$ and $x = 2$.

Solution: Let $f(x) = x^3 – x – 1$. It is clear that $f$ is continuous on the interval $[1, 2]$. Also, $f(1) = -1 < 0$ and $f(2) = 5 > 0$. Therefore, by using the above corollary, the equation $x^3 – x – 1 = 0$ has at least one solution between $x = 1$ and $x = 2$.

Practical problem:

Use the intermediate value theorem to show that among all circles with radius no larger than 10 cm., there is one whose area is $200cm^2$.

Solution: The area $A(r)$ of a circle with radius $r$ is given by $A(r) = \pi r^2$. Since $A(r)$ is a continuous function on the interval $[0, 10]$ and for $r = 0$, $A(r) = 0$, and for $r = 10$, $A(r) = 100\pi \approx 314$. Since $200$ lies between $0$ and $314$, by the intermediate value theorem, there is an $r$ between $0$ and $10$ such that $A(r) = 200$.
