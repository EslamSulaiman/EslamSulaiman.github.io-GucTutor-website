Chapter 2: Engineering Materials

Mechanical Properties of Metals

Mechanical properties determine a material's behavior under applied forces. These properties are crucial for selecting the right material for a specific engineering application.

1. Strength

  • Definition: A material's ability to withstand an applied stress without failure. Stress is defined as force per unit area.
  • Types of Strength:
    • Yield Strength (Yield Point): The stress at which a material begins to deform plastically (permanently). Beyond this point, the material will not return to its original shape after the load is removed.
    • Ultimate Tensile Strength (UTS): The maximum stress a material can withstand before necking (localized reduction in cross-sectional area) begins, leading to fracture.
    • Fracture Strength: The stress at which the material actually breaks.

2. Hardness

  • Definition: A material's resistance to permanent indentation, scratching, or abrasion. It is often related to tensile strength.
  • Importance: Hardness is an important property in applications where materials are subjected to wear or localized deformation, such as gears, bearings, and cutting tools.
  • Measurement Methods:
    • Brinell Hardness Test (HB): Uses a hardened steel or tungsten carbide ball indentor. Suitable for softer to medium-hard materials.
    • Rockwell Hardness Test (HR): Uses a cone or ball indentor. Different scales (e.g., HRC, HRB) for various material types and hardness ranges. Quick and widely used.
    • Vickers Hardness Test (HV): Uses a diamond pyramid indentor. Suitable for very hard materials and thin sections.
    • Knoop Hardness Test (HK): Uses a diamond pyramidal indentor, often used for very small or brittle materials.

3. Ductility

  • Definition: The ability of a material to deform plastically under tensile stress without fracturing. It is the ability to be drawn into a wire.
  • Measurement: Often quantified by percentage elongation and percentage reduction in area in a tensile test.
  • Importance: Ductile materials can be shaped by processes like drawing, forging, and extrusion. They also tend to absorb more energy before fracture, making them safer in many applications.

4. Brittleness

  • Definition: The opposite of ductility. A brittle material exhibits little or no plastic deformation before fracture. It breaks suddenly under stress.
  • Examples: Cast iron, ceramics, glass.
  • Importance: Brittle materials are generally not suitable for applications where shock loading or significant deformation is expected.

5. Malleability

  • Definition: The ability of a material to deform plastically under compressive stress without fracturing. It is the ability to be hammered or rolled into thin sheets.
  • Importance: Malleable materials are suitable for processes like rolling and forging.

6. Toughness

  • Definition: The ability of a material to absorb energy and deform plastically before fracturing. It represents the resistance to crack propagation.
  • Measurement: Represented by the area under the stress-strain curve up to fracture. Also measured by impact tests (e.g., Charpy or Izod tests).
  • Importance: Tough materials are desirable in applications subjected to impact or sudden loads.

7. Stiffness (Modulus of Elasticity, Young's Modulus - E)

  • Definition: A material's resistance to elastic deformation under stress. It is the ratio of stress to strain in the elastic region of the stress-strain curve.
  • Formula: $E = \frac{\text{Stress}}{\text{Strain}} = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon}$
  • Importance: A higher modulus of elasticity indicates a stiffer material, meaning it deforms less under a given load within its elastic limit.

8. Elasticity

  • Definition: The ability of a material to return to its original shape and size after the applied load is removed. This deformation is temporary and non-permanent.

9. Plasticity

  • Definition: The ability of a material to undergo permanent (plastic) deformation without fracture when subjected to stress beyond its elastic limit.

10. Creep

  • Definition: Time-dependent plastic deformation of a material under a constant load at elevated temperatures.

11. Fatigue

  • Definition: Failure of a material under repeated or cyclic loading, even if the applied stresses are below the material's yield strength.
