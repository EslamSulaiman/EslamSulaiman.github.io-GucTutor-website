Shift Formulas: Shifting Functions

Vertical Shifts

    [cite_start]
  • For $y=f(x)+k$: Shifts the graph of $f$ up $k$ units if $k>0$. [cite: 1]
    • [cite_start]
  • Shifts it down $|k|$ units if $k<0$. [cite: 1]

Example: Vertical Shift
Consider the basic function $y=x^2$.
- $y=x^2+1$ shifts the graph of $y=x^2$ up by 1 unit.
- [cite_start]$y=x^2-2$ shifts the graph of $y=x^2$ down by 2 units. [cite: 1]

Horizontal Shifts

    [cite_start]
  • For $y=f(x+h)$: Shifts the graph of $f$ left $h$ units if $h>0$. [cite: 1]
    • [cite_start]
  • Shifts it right $|h|$ units if $h<0$. [cite: 1]

Example: Horizontal Shift
Consider the basic function $y=x^2$.
- $y=(x+3)^2$ shifts the graph of $y=x^2$ to the left by 3 units.
- [cite_start]$y=(x-2)^2$ shifts the graph of $y=x^2$ to the right by 2 units. [cite: 2]

Curve Sketching: Very Important Steps

[cite_start]

To sketch the graph of a transformed function, follow these steps: [cite: 3]

    [cite_start]
  1. Sketch the graph of the basic function. [cite: 3]
    2. [cite_start]
  2. Reflect it around the y-axis, if needed. [cite: 4]
    3. [cite_start]
  3. Make horizontal shifts "right or left", if needed. [cite: 4]
    4. [cite_start]
  4. Reflect it around the x-axis, if needed. [cite: 5]
    5. [cite_start]
  5. Make vertical shifts "up or down", if needed. [cite: 5]
[cite_start]

Note: It is generally better to proceed by first reflecting, then shifting. [cite: 6]

Examples:

Sketch a) $f(x)=-\sqrt{2x+4}-3$

[cite_start]

This can be rewritten as $f(x)=-\sqrt{2(x+2)}-3$. [cite: 6]

    [cite_start]
  • Basic function: $y=\sqrt{x}$. [cite: 6]
    • [cite_start]
  • Step 1: Reflection across the X-axis: Apply the negative sign outside the square root, $y=-\sqrt{x}$. [cite: 6]
  • Step 2: Horizontal Shift (due to $x+2$): Shift left by 2 units. This results from $x+2$, so for the general form $f(x+h)$, here $h=2 > 0$ means a left shift. [cite_start]This implies a transformation from $\sqrt{2x}$ to $\sqrt{2(x+2)}$. [cite: 6]
    • [cite_start]
  • Step 3: Vertical Shift (due to $-3$): Shift down by 3 units. [cite: 6]

Sketch b) $f(x)=-3x^{2}+6x-1$

[cite_start]

First, complete the square to get $f(x)=-3(x-1)^{2}+2$. [cite: 6]

    [cite_start]
  • Basic function: $y=x^2$. [cite: 7]
  • Step 1: Reflection across the x-axis and Vertical Stretch: Apply the $-3$ factor, $y=-3x^2$. [cite_start]The negative sign reflects across the x-axis, and the '3' causes a vertical stretch. [cite: 6]
    • [cite_start]
  • Step 2: Horizontal Shift (due to $x-1$): Shift right by 1 unit. [cite: 7]
    • [cite_start]
  • Step 3: Vertical Shift (due to $+2$): Shift up by 2 units. [cite: 7]

3. Rational function

[cite_start]

Definition: The quotient of two polynomials is called a rational function $f(x) = \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$, where $P(x)$ and $Q(x)$ are polynomials. [cite: 11]

The domain of the rational function is $\mathbb{R} - \{x | [cite_start]Q(x)=0\}$ (all real numbers except where the denominator is zero). [cite: 12]

[cite_start]

Example: The domain of the rational function $f(x)=\frac{3x^2-1}{x-4}$ is $\mathbb{R}-\{4\}$, which can also be written as $\{x : x \in \mathbb{R}, x \ne 4\}$. [cite: 12]

Some special rational functions:
- The graph for $y=\frac{1}{x^n}$ will have a shape similar to $y=\frac{1}{x}$ when $n$ is odd (branches in opposite quadrants).
- [cite_start]The graph for $y=\frac{1}{x^n}$ will have a shape similar to $y=\frac{1}{x^2}$ when $n$ is even (branches in adjacent quadrants, typically both going to positive or negative infinity). [cite: 13]

Example: Sketch the graph of $y = -\frac{1}{(x-1)^2} + 3$

[cite_start]

Apply the 3 steps of curve sketching: [cite: 13]

  1. Start with the basic graph of $y=\frac{1}{x^2}$.
    2. [cite_start]
  2. Reflect on the x-axis (due to the negative sign in front): $y=-\frac{1}{x^2}$. [cite: 13]
    3. [cite_start]
  3. Shift right by 1 unit (due to $x-1$): $y=-\frac{1}{(x-1)^2}$. [cite: 13]
    4. [cite_start]
  4. Shift up by 3 units (due to $+3$): $y=-\frac{1}{(x-1)^2}+3$. [cite: 13]

The absolute value function $f(x)=|x|$ has a domain of $(-\infty, \infty)$ and a range of $[0, \infty)$. [cite: 14]

  • Shift the basic graph of $y=|x|$ 2 units to the right (due to $x-2$). [cite: 15]

5. Exponential function

[cite_start]

Definition: For any positive real number $a$ and any real number $x$, we define the exponential function as $f(x) = a^x$. [cite: 15]

[cite_start]

Special cases: [cite: 16, 17]

    [cite_start]
  • If $a=1$, then $f(x)=1^x=1$. [cite: 16]
    • [cite_start]
  • If $a=0$, then $f(x)=0^x=0$, provided $x>0$. [cite: 17]
[cite_start]

Examples: Use the ideas of reflection and shifting to sketch the graph of: [cite: 18]

  1. $f(x) = 4^{x+2}$
    [cite_start]The graph of $y=4^x$ shifted to the left by 2 units. [cite: 18]
  2. $f(x) = 4^x + 2$
    [cite_start]The graph of $y=4^x$ shifted up by 2 units. [cite: 18]
  3. $f(x) = 3 - 4^{-x} = -4^{-x} + 3$
    [cite_start]The graph of $y=4^x$ will be: [cite: 18]
      [cite_start]
    • Reflected on the y-axis (due to $-x$). [cite: 18]
      • [cite_start]
    • Reflected on the x-axis (due to the negative sign in front of $4^{-x}$). [cite: 19]
      • [cite_start]
    • Shifted up by 3 units (due to $+3$). [cite: 19]

Special case: The natural exponential function $f(x) = e^x$

[cite_start]

A very special irrational number denoted by $e \approx 2.71828...$ is very useful in engineering applications. [cite: 19]

The function $f(x)=e^x$ is called the natural exponential function. [cite_start]Its graph is shown in the figure (similar to $y=a^x$ for $a>1$). [cite: 20]

[cite_start]

Properties of the natural exponential function: [cite: 21]

  • $e^2 \cdot e^3 = e^5$
  • $e^{-3} = 1/e^3$
  • $e^5 / e^3 = e^2$
  • $(e^2)^3 = e^6$

6. Logarithmic function

[cite_start]

We define $y = \log_a(x)$ if and only if $x = a^y$. [cite: 22]

[cite_start]

We can think of "$\log_a(x)$" as the power of "$a$" that gives $x$. [cite: 22]

[cite_start]

Examples: [cite: 21]

  • $\log_2(32) = 5$, because $2^5 = 32$. [cite_start]This means the power of 2 that gives 32 is 5. [cite: 21]
  • $\log_2(2) = 1$, because $2^1 = 2$. [cite_start]The power of 2 that gives 2 is 1. [cite: 21]
  • $\log_2(1) = 0$, because $2^0 = 1$. [cite_start]The power of 2 that gives 1 is 0. [cite: 21]

Graph of the logarithmic function:

[cite_start]

The graph of $y=\log_a(x)$ is the reflection of $y=a^x$ on the line $y=x$. [cite: 23]
