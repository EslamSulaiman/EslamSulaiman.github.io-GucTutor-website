Area under the Curve

We start by asking: How can we find the area of a region with a curved boundary?

We consider a continuous and non-negative function $y=f(x)$ over a closed interval $[a, b]$. We want to find the area of the region bounded by $y=f(x)$, the x-axis, and the vertical lines $x=a$ and $x=b$.

Steps to approximate the area:

  1. Divide the interval $[a, b]$ into $n$ subintervals of equal width $\Delta x = \frac{b-a}{n}$. These subintervals are $[x_0, x_1], [x_1, x_2], \dots, [x_{n-1}, x_n]$, where $x_0=a$ and $x_n=b$.
  2. On each subinterval, construct a rectangle whose height is determined by the function value at a chosen point within that subinterval (e.g., left endpoint, right endpoint, or midpoint).
  3. Sum the areas of these $n$ rectangles. This sum is called a Riemann Sum.

Example: Right-endpoint sum The height of the rectangle on the $k$-th subinterval $[x_{k-1}, x_k]$ is $f(x_k)$. The area of the $k$-th rectangle is $A_k = f(x_k)\Delta x$. The approximate total area is $A \approx \sum_{k=1}^{n} f(x_k)\Delta x$.

To get the exact area, we take the limit as the number of rectangles approaches infinity: $$A = \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{k=1}^{n} f(x_k)\Delta x$$

2. The Definite Integral

The limit of the Riemann sum (if it exists) is called the definite integral of $f$ from $a$ to $b$, and is denoted by $\int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx$. $$ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx = \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{k=1}^{n} f(x_k^*)\Delta x $$ where $x_k^*$ is any sample point in the $k$-th subinterval.

  • $a$: lower limit of integration.
  • $b$: upper limit of integration.
  • $\int$: integral sign.
  • $f(x)$: integrand.
  • $dx$: differential (indicates variable of integration).

Note: The definite integral is a number, while the indefinite integral is a family of functions.

Properties of Definite Integrals:

  • Order of Integration: $\int_{b}^{a} f(x) dx = -\int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx$.
  • Zero Width Interval: $\int_{a}^{a} f(x) dx = 0$.
  • Constant Multiple: $\int_{a}^{b} k f(x) dx = k \int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx$.
  • Sum and Difference: $\int_{a}^{b} [f(x)\pm g(x)] dx = \int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx \pm \int_{a}^{b} g(x) dx$.
  • Additivity: $\int_{a}^{c} f(x) dx + \int_{c}^{b} f(x) dx = \int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx$.
  • Max-Min Inequality: If $min f \le f(x) \le max f$ on $[a,b]$, then $min f \cdot (b-a) \le \int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx \le max f \cdot (b-a)$.
  • Domination:
    • If $f(x)\ge g(x)$ on $[a, b]$, then $\int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx \ge \int_{a}^{b} g(x) dx$.
    • If $f(x)\ge 0$ on $[a, b]$, then $\int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx \ge 0$.

3. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC)

Part 1 (Relationship between differentiation and integration):

If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$, then the function $F(x)=\int_{a}^{x} f(t) dt$ has a derivative at every point $x$ in $[a, b]$, and $$ \frac{d}{dx} \int_{a}^{x} f(t) dt = f(x) $$

This means that differentiation "undoes" integration.

Examples:

1. Find $\frac{dy}{dx}$ if $y=\int_{0}^{x} \sqrt{1+t^{2}} dt$.
Solution: $\frac{dy}{dx}=\sqrt{1+x^{2}}$.

2. Find $\frac{dy}{dx}$ if $y=\int_{x}^{5} (t\sin t) dt$.
Solution: $y=-\int_{5}^{x} (t\sin t) dt \Rightarrow \frac{dy}{dx}=-(x\sin x)$.

3. Find $\frac{dy}{dx}$ if $y=\int_{0}^{x^{2}} \cos(t^{2}) dt$.
Let $u=x^{2}$, then $y=\int_{0}^{u} \cos(t^{2}) dt$.
Using the Chain Rule, $\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{dy}{du}\frac{du}{dx}$.
$\frac{dy}{du}=\cos(u^{2})=\cos((x^{2})^{2})=\cos(x^{4})$.
$\frac{du}{dx}=2x$.
So, $\frac{dy}{dx}=\cos(x^{4})\cdot 2x$.

Part 2 (The Evaluation Theorem):

If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ and $F$ is any antiderivative of $f$ on $[a, b]$, then $$ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx = F(b)-F(a) $$ This part provides a method for evaluating definite integrals without using the limit of Riemann sums.

Examples:

1. Evaluate $\int_{-1}^{2} x^{2} dx$.
Solution: An antiderivative of $x^{2}$ is $\frac{x^{3}}{3}$.
$\int_{-1}^{2} x^{2} dx = \left[\frac{x^{3}}{3}\right]_{-1}^{2} = \frac{2^{3}}{3}-\frac{(-1)^{3}}{3}=\frac{8}{3}-(-\frac{1}{3})=\frac{8}{3}+\frac{1}{3}=\frac{9}{3}=3$.

2. Evaluate $\int_{0}^{\pi/2} \sin x dx$.
Solution: An antiderivative of $\sin x$ is $-\cos x$.
$\int_{0}^{\pi/2} \sin x dx = [-\cos x]_{0}^{\pi/2} = -\cos(\frac{\pi}{2})-(-\cos(0)) = -0-(-1)=1$.

3. Evaluate $\int_{0}^{1} e^{x} dx$.
Solution: An antiderivative of $e^{x}$ is $e^{x}$.
$\int_{0}^{1} e^{x} dx = [e^{x}]_{0}^{1} = e^{1}-e^{0}=e-1$.
