Physics 101 Lecture 11 (Chapter 10): Rotational Motion of Rigid Bodies - Part I

Rotational Kinetics - Moment of Inertia

This lecture, presented by Dr. Ahmed Maarouf, introduces the fundamental concepts of rotational motion for rigid bodies, focusing on rotational kinematics, moment of inertia, torque, and rotational kinetic energy. [cite_start]It draws parallels between linear and rotational quantities and provides essential formulas and examples for analyzing rotating systems. [cite: 174, 175, 177, 179]

11.1 Rigid Body Definition

[cite_start]A **rigid object (body)** is a non-deformable object whose dimensions do not change during its motion, and the relative locations of all its constituent particles remain constant. [cite: 261, 262, 263] [cite_start]This model simplifies the analysis of extended solid objects, especially when deformation is negligible. [cite: 259, 260, 264]

11.2 Rotational Kinematics: Angular Quantities

Angular Position ($\theta$)

[cite_start]The angular position of a rigid object is defined as the angle between a reference line attached to the object and a reference line fixed in space (often the x-axis). [cite: 181, 268, 269, 270, 274, 282, 310, 311] [cite_start]It plays the same role in rotational motion as translational position ($x$) in linear motion. [cite: 312] [cite_start]Polar coordinates $(r, \theta)$ are convenient for representing the position of a point on a rotating disc, where $r$ is the distance from the origin $O$ to the point $P$, and $\theta$ is measured counterclockwise from the reference line. [cite: 271, 273, 275, 276, 277, 278]

As a particle at $P$ moves through an angle $\theta$, it sweeps an arc length $s$ on a circular path of radius $r$. [cite_start]These quantities are related by: [cite: 294, 295, 296, 298] \[ s = r\theta \quad \text{or} \quad \theta = \frac{s}{r} \] [cite_start]Here, $s$ and $r$ are in meters, and $\theta$ is measured in **radians**. [cite: 296, 298, 303] [cite_start]One radian is the angle subtended by an arc whose length equals the radius of the arc. [cite: 304] \[ 1 \, \text{rad} = \frac{360^\circ}{2\pi} \approx 57.3^\circ \] [cite_start]\[ \theta(\text{rad}) = \frac{\pi}{180^\circ} \theta(\text{deg}) \] [cite: 306, 307]

Angular Displacement ($\Delta\theta$)

[cite_start]The angular displacement is the angle an object rotates through during a time interval. [cite: 182, 313] [cite_start]It is the angle swept out by the reference line of length $r$: [cite: 314] [cite_start]\[ \Delta\theta \equiv \theta_f - \theta_i \] [cite: 182, 315] [cite_start]Every particle in a rigid object rotates through the same angular displacement. [cite: 189, 315, 142]

Angular Velocity ($\omega$)

[cite_start]The **average angular velocity** ($\omega_{avg}$) is the ratio of angular displacement to the time interval: [cite: 316] [cite_start]\[ \omega_{avg} = \frac{\Delta\theta}{\Delta t} = \frac{\theta_f - \theta_i}{t_f - t_i} \] [cite: 316] [cite_start]The **instantaneous angular speed** ($\omega$) is the limit of the average angular speed as $\Delta t$ approaches zero: [cite: 183, 184, 318, 319] [cite_start]\[ \omega \equiv \frac{d\theta}{dt} \] [cite: 184] [cite_start]Units of angular velocity are radians/second (rad/s or rad·s⁻¹). [cite: 184, 317] [cite_start]Angular velocity $\omega$ is positive for counterclockwise rotation ($\theta$ increasing) and negative for clockwise rotation ($\theta$ decreasing). [cite: 320, 145]

Angular Acceleration ($\alpha$)

[cite_start]The **average angular acceleration** ($\alpha_{avg}$) is the ratio of the change in angular velocity to the time taken: [cite: 322] [cite_start]\[ \alpha_{avg} = \frac{\Delta\omega}{\Delta t} = \frac{\omega_f - \omega_i}{t_f - t_i} \] [cite: 322] [cite_start]The **instantaneous angular acceleration** ($\alpha$) is the limit of the average angular acceleration as $\Delta t$ approaches zero (the time derivative of $\omega$): [cite: 186, 187, 323] [cite_start]\[ \alpha \equiv \frac{d\omega}{dt} \] [cite: 187] [cite_start]Units of angular acceleration are radians/second squared (rad/s²). [cite: 187, 323] [cite_start]Angular acceleration $\alpha$ is positive if an object rotating counterclockwise is speeding up, and negative if it is slowing down while rotating counterclockwise. [cite: 324] [cite_start]For a rigid object rotating about a fixed axis, every part of the object has the same angular speed and angular acceleration. [cite: 189, 142]

Rotational Kinematic Equations (Constant Angular Acceleration)

[cite_start]When a rigid object rotates about a fixed axis under constant angular acceleration, kinematic equations analogous to those for translational motion can be applied. [cite: 113, 114, 239, 145, 146] [cite_start]The substitutions are $x \to \theta$, $v \to \omega$, and $a \to \alpha$. [cite: 146]

    [cite_start]
  • Angular velocity as a function of time: [cite: 114, 240] \[ \omega_f = \omega_i + \alpha t \]
    • [cite_start]
  • Angular position as a function of time: [cite: 114, 241] \[ \theta_f = \theta_i + \omega_i t + \frac{1}{2}\alpha t^2 \]
    • [cite_start]
  • Angular velocity squared as a function of angular displacement: [cite: 114, 241] \[ \omega_f^2 = \omega_i^2 + 2\alpha(\theta_f - \theta_i) \]
    • [cite_start]
  • Angular position as a function of initial and final angular velocities and time: [cite: 114, 241] \[ \theta_f = \theta_i + \frac{1}{2}(\omega_i + \omega_f)t \]

11.3 Relation Between Linear and Angular Quantities

[cite_start]While every point on a rotating rigid body has the same angular motion ($\theta, \omega, \alpha$), different points may have different linear motions ($s, v, a$) because the linear quantities depend on the distance $r$ from the axis of rotation. [cite: 147, 148, 149, 150, 151]

    [cite_start]
  • Arc Length (translational position): [cite: 112, 220, 149] \[ s = r\theta \]
    • [cite_start]
  • Tangential Speed (translational speed): The linear velocity is always tangent to the circular path. [cite: 112, 222, 149] \[ v = r\omega \] [cite_start]The tangential speed increases with distance $r$ from the center of rotation. [cite: 151]
    • [cite_start]
  • Tangential Acceleration: [cite: 112, 225, 152] \[ a_t = r\alpha \]
    • [cite_start]
  • Centripetal (Radial) Acceleration: Any object moving in a circle has a centripetal acceleration directed towards the center. [cite: 153] [cite_start]\[ a_r = \frac{v^2}{r} = r\omega^2 \] [cite: 154]
    • [cite_start]
  • Total Linear Acceleration: The tangential component is due to changing speed, and the centripetal component is due to changing direction. [cite: 155] [cite_start]\[ a = \sqrt{a_t^2 + a_r^2} = \sqrt{(r\alpha)^2 + (r\omega^2)^2} = r\sqrt{\alpha^2 + \omega^4} \] [cite: 156]

11.4 Moment of Inertia ($I$)

[cite_start]When an object rotates about an axis with angular speed $\omega$, it possesses rotational kinetic energy. [cite: 156] Each particle in the object has kinetic energy $K_i = \frac{1}{2}m_i v_i^2$. [cite_start]Since $v_i = r_i\omega$, the total rotational kinetic energy is found by summing over all particles. [cite: 157]

Definition of Moment of Inertia

[cite_start]The **moment of inertia ($I$)** is a measure of an object's resistance to changes in its rotational motion. [cite: 163] [cite_start]It depends on the mass of the object and how that mass is distributed around the axis of rotation. [cite: 162, 164]

[cite_start]For a system of particles, the moment of inertia is defined as: [cite: 108, 197, 164] \[ I \equiv \sum_i m_i r_i^2 \] [cite_start]Where $m_i$ is the mass of the $i^{th}$ particle and $r_i$ is its perpendicular distance from the rotation axis. [cite: 198]

[cite_start]For a continuous rigid object, the moment of inertia is found by integrating over all mass elements: [cite: 111, 211, 212, 161, 164, 165] \[ I = \int r^2 dm \] [cite_start]Where $r$ is the distance from the mass element $dm$ to the axis of rotation. [cite: 213]

    [cite_start]
  • Dimensions: $ML^2$ [cite: 160]
    • [cite_start]
  • SI Units: kg·m² [cite: 160]

[cite_start]Different mass distributions can be described using densities: [cite: 166, 167]

  • **Volume mass density ($\rho$):** mass per unit volume ($dm = \rho \, dV$)
  • **Surface mass density ($\sigma$):** mass per unit area ($dm = \sigma \, dA$)
  • **Linear mass density ($\lambda$):** mass per unit length ($dm = \lambda \, dL$)

Parallel Axis Theorem

[cite_start]The parallel-axis theorem simplifies the calculation of the moment of inertia for an arbitrary axis that is parallel to an axis passing through the center of mass. [cite: 168, 169] [cite_start]\[ I = I_{CM} + MD^2 \] [cite: 169] Where:

    [cite_start]
  • $I$ is the moment of inertia about the arbitrary axis. [cite: 169]
    • [cite_start]
  • $I_{CM}$ is the moment of inertia about the axis passing through the center of mass. [cite: 170]
    • [cite_start]
  • $M$ is the total mass of the body. [cite: 172]
    • [cite_start]
  • $D$ is the perpendicular distance between the center of mass axis and the arbitrary parallel axis. [cite: 171]

Example: Moment of Inertia of a Rod Rotating Around One End

[cite_start]If the moment of inertia of a uniform rod of mass $M$ and length $L$ about an axis through its midpoint (CM) is $I_{CM} = \frac{1}{12}ML^2$, [cite: 172] [cite_start]then the moment of inertia about an axis passing through one of its ends (where $D = L/2$) is: [cite: 172] [cite_start]\[ I = I_{CM} + MD^2 = \frac{1}{12}ML^2 + M\left(\frac{L}{2}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{12}ML^2 + \frac{1}{4}ML^2 = \frac{1}{3}ML^2 \] [cite: 172]

11.5 Rotational Kinetic Energy ($K_R$)

[cite_start]If a rigid object rotates about a fixed axis with angular speed $\omega$, its rotational kinetic energy is: [cite: 110, 206] [cite_start]\[ K_R = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 \] [cite: 207] [cite_start]Where $I$ is the moment of inertia about the axis of rotation. [cite: 209, 158]

[cite_start]For a rigid object rolling on a rough surface without slipping, its total kinetic energy ($K$) is the sum of its rotational kinetic energy about its center of mass ($I_{CM}$) and the translational kinetic energy of its center of mass ($M v_{CM}^2$): [cite: 113, 231, 232] [cite_start]\[ K = \frac{1}{2}I_{CM}\omega^2 + \frac{1}{2}Mv_{CM}^2 \] [cite: 232]

11.6 Torque ($\tau$)

**Torque** is the rotational equivalent of force; it is what causes an object to rotate or change its rotational motion.

Definition of Torque

[cite_start]The magnitude of the torque associated with a force $\vec{F}$ acting on an object at a distance $r$ from the rotation axis is: [cite: 109, 199, 200] [cite_start]\[ \tau = rF \sin\phi = Fd \] [cite: 200] Where:

    [cite_start]
  • $r$ is the distance from the rotation axis to the point where the force is applied. [cite: 199]
    • [cite_start]
  • $\phi$ is the angle between the position vector $\vec{r}$ (from the axis to the point of application) and the force vector $\vec{F}$. [cite: 202]
    • [cite_start]
  • $d = r \sin\phi$ is the **moment arm** (or lever arm) of the force, which is the perpendicular distance from the rotation axis to the line of action of the force. [cite: 202]

Newton's Second Law for Rotation (Rigid Object Under a Net Torque)

If a rigid object free to rotate about a fixed axis has a net external torque acting on it, the object undergoes an angular acceleration $\alpha$. [cite_start]This relationship is the rotational analog to Newton's Second Law ($\sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}$). [cite: 115, 252, 253, 255] [cite_start]\[ \sum\tau_{ext} = I\alpha \] [cite: 253]

11.7 Work and Power in Rotational Motion

Work Done by a Torque

[cite_start]If work is done on a rigid object and the only result of the work is rotation about a fixed axis, the net work done by external forces in rotating the object equals the change in its rotational kinetic energy: [cite: 112, 113, 229, 230] [cite_start]\[ \sum W = \frac{1}{2}I\omega_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}I\omega_i^2 \] [cite: 230]

Power Delivered by a Torque

[cite_start]The rate at which work is done by an external force in rotating a rigid object about a fixed axis, or the power delivered, is: [cite: 112, 226, 227, 228] [cite_start]\[ P = \tau\omega \] [cite: 228]
